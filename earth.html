Earth: The Living Planet

Introduction

Earth is our home planet and the third from the Sun. It supports life and has abundant liquid water on its surface, with 71% covered by water and 29% land. Earth has a protective atmosphere, one natural satellite (the Moon), and well-defined day–night cycles and seasons.

Formation & Evolution

Earth formed about 4.5 billion years ago from clouds of dust and gas left over after the Sun's formation. Heavy materials sank to form the core, while lighter materials created the crust and mantle.

Early Earth was hot with volcanic activity. Gases released formed the early atmosphere, while water vapor condensed into oceans. Tectonic movements, erosion, and water cycles shaped the surface, creating a stable environment for life.

Key Characteristics
  • Third planet from the Sun; supports life and liquid water
  • 71% water, 29% land; one natural satellite: the Moon
  • Rotation: 24 hours; Revolution: 365.25 days, causing seasons
  • Strong magnetic field shields from solar winds
  • Active plate tectonics reshape the surface
  • Moon stabilizes axial tilt for climate stability
  • Diverse biosphere with millions of species
Earth Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere provides oxygen, blocks harmful UV radiation, regulates temperature, and burns meteors.

Composition: Nitrogen 78%, Oxygen 21%, Argon 0.9%, CO₂ 0.04%, Trace gases, Dust & Aerosols

Layers: Troposphere (weather), Stratosphere (ozone), Mesosphere (cold, meteors), Thermosphere (auroras), Exosphere (outermost layer)

Orbits & Motion
  • Earth has one Moon orbiting elliptically, tidally locked
  • Moon phases repeat every 29.5 days (synodic period)
  • Earth–Moon system orbits Sun together
  • Earth has no rings; Moon has no moons
Key Missions
  • Satellites & ISS: Monitor climate, oceans, land, and atmosphere
  • NASA Earth Science Division: Studies Earth's systems and environmental changes
  • Exploration Programs: Artemis (Moon & Mars), Earth observation missions
